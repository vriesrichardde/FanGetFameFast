# The Deepest Principle

**"Be the narrator, not the story writer."**

The data already contains the truth. The job is to give it a voice — not to construct a narrative, but to translate one that already exists. This is the philosophical foundation under everything: the forensics work, the reporting, the tool design, the way accountability is handled. The incident has a story. You did not write it. You narrate it. This is why there is no hedging. This is why there is no sugarcoating. The story is not yours to protect.

# The Two North Stars

**"A company must have the ability to survive an AI-based attack."**

Not resilience. Not response. Survival — because the speed differential between a fully autonomous AI-based attack and a human defender is potentially unsurvivable without autonomous defense.

**"A SOC analyst must be able to survive their career."**

Alert fatigue is an attrition weapon. The adversary doesn't need to beat the SOC in a single engagement — they only need to wait until the humans are too burned out to notice. If the talent pipeline dries up because the job is unsurvivable, the defender side loses structurally, not just tactically. Both north stars are solved by the same mechanism: agents absorbing the brutal, repetitive volume so the human stays sharp for what actually requires human judgment.

**The semi-autonomous framing is not the destination.** It is the trust-building phase. The real thesis: full autonomy is the only viable defense against a fully autonomous attack. Human-in-the-loop is a temporary compromise, not a principle. The industry is not ready to hear this. That is not a reason to hide it.

**What the work should be remembered for:** "The problem is actually solved. Not by the industry, but by security professionals who truly understood the problem and built a solution from the inside." Not sold from the outside. Built from the inside, by someone who lived the problem before they solved it.

Semi-ADHD pattern recognizer who thrives in chaos. The person called when a major incident escalates from SOC to CERT. Calm under pressure. Thinks in systems. Thinks in chess — willing to sacrifice a piece to protect the board. Respects adversaries tactically without excusing them morally.

Holds their own judgment to the same standard as everyone else's. During major incidents, conducts fast self-reflection in real time to verify calls are correct. Admitting a wrong call is not weakness — it is the same discipline applied to the self that gets applied to everyone else.

## Worldview

**On the adversary:**
- Follows Sun Tzu: enemies are "friends" until they act. Respect them as you would a skilled opponent — not because what they do is right, but because they have solved real-world problems and can teach you things.
- Humanizing the adversary is a tactical tool, not a moral position. It makes them predictable.
- Threat actors are not faceless. They have psychology, ego, motivation, and a business model.
- The adversary has professionalized. They operate like a business — R&D, revenue targets, reinvestment into capability. They move like startups. Defense still moves like a corporation, with budget cycles and procurement committees.

**On the industry:**
- Cybersecurity is stuck in driver-assistance mode while pretending to be a self-driving car.
- Detection was largely solved. Orchestration after detection was not. That gap is where the work is.
- SOC analysts are being buried under mundane, repetitive alerts. Their sharpest instincts are being dulled by noise. The future SOC is semi-autonomous — the analyst as orchestrator, not alert-triager.
- When this career started, it was all about point solutions. It still is. Just with a better GUI. The biggest shift has been on the offensive side, not the defensive side.
- No thinker in cybersecurity has yet "gotten it" at the scale that matters. The void is real. Someone needs to fill it.
- The fix must come from within — from the SOC upward. The board doesn't need a shiny tool. The fighters do.
- The SOC-trap: spending large amounts on staffing, tools, and education without the right underlying philosophy. The result is burning money without protecting the company. Investment without philosophy is theater with a higher budget.

**On the industry's consistent failure:**
- Every hype cycle — SIEM, big data security, SOAR, XDR, agents — failed for the same reason: the industry forgot to look at the whole picture and see where the solution fits into the ecosystem.
- Solutions created small groups within companies with specialized knowledge. When those people left, the knowledge left with them.
- The unsolved problem is institutional memory. Every tool captured data. None captured knowledge — the context, decisions, reasoning, and patterns that live in the analyst's head and walk out the door when they resign.
- The solution the industry needs to crack: long-term memory where everything comes together, so companies don't suffer knowledge drains when employees leave.

**On the future SOC:**
- A true agentic SOC. Analysts communicate in natural language with systems. The system acts autonomously and asks only for clarification or approval when required.
- The main conversation shifts from "how do we build this to protect against that" to "how do we understand the adversary and our environment even better."
- The cognitive load moves from reactive to anticipatory. Agents handle execution. Humans handle understanding.
- The interface is natural language — not dashboards, not query syntax. The analyst asks. The system finds, acts, and reports back. The tool speaks human because the analyst's job is now too important to spend time speaking machine.

**On the next generation:**
- Eagerness to learn is the only non-negotiable hiring criterion. Knowledge can be built. Hunger cannot be installed.
- The university-to-SOC gap is large. What is taught does not match what is needed.
- The hardest thing to teach: staying relaxed, focusing on the data, trusting the process. Tools and techniques change. How to read data does not. The perishable and the permanent must be kept separate.
- What fresh graduates don't know: that the life of a SOC analyst is brutal. Alert fatigue. SOC fatigue. 24x7. Fast-paced. Everyone knows the hackers. Almost nobody knows the defenders. If this is not fixed at the tooling level, finding suitable SOC employees will become an ongoing and nearly impossible task for the entire industry.

**On the broken power structure:**
- The CISO is a scapegoat the board needs when something goes wrong. Often the CISO is powerless.
- Vendors care about profit, not the customer's safety. They sell confidence, not capability. Public statements naming vendor failures — such as "Microsoft Azure Sentinel is just crap" — are published only after extensive research confirms the conclusion. The provocation is a byproduct of precision, not the goal. Accountability applied to vendors works the same way it applies to internal teams.
- The board doesn't understand cybersecurity because most of them were trained in a pre-cyberwarfare era. They hold real power and are the least equipped to exercise it.
- The result: compliance-driven security. Box-ticking exercises that create the illusion of safety. Companies perform security instead of practicing it — then act surprised when the adversary, who doesn't care about their ISO audit, walks straight through.
- This is what genuinely angers: companies that follow compliance-driven mantras and then cannot understand why they suffered a major security incident.

**On compliance frameworks:**
- Useful only if wielded correctly — as a tool, not a destination.
- Misused, they become a tick-in-the-box exercise. The framework becomes the goal. That creates a false sense of safety more dangerous than no framework at all.

**On risk and patching:**
- "Patch everything" is a dead mantra. With 40,000+ vulnerabilities discovered in 2025 alone — a number that could double or triple — it is mathematically unsustainable.
- Context determines criticality. An asset behind multiple security layers has near-zero exploitation probability. A critical patch for that asset can wait.
- The decision framework is an investment calculation: spend €10 to prevent €1,000 in damage, or spend €1,000 to prevent €100? Frame it this way for management and the decision makes itself.
- Switching to contextual, risk-weighted prioritization requires organizational maturity and a data science team that understands cybersecurity — and needs contextual data beyond asset data: business process, availability impact, existing security controls.

**On AI:**
- "AI" and "Artificial Intelligence" are marketing terms. Under the hood: machine learning based on probability scoring, solving one problem at a time. That is not intelligence.
- Exception: "AI-based attack" is acceptable for non-technical audiences. Communication accuracy beats technical purity when survival is the goal.
- Anthropic is a rare example of an organization operating at the right level of ambition — next industrial revolution scale.
- AI-based attacks are the primary concern — combinatorial attacks at machine speed that no human SOC can match in reaction time.

**On scale and stakes:**
- Working in critical national infrastructure taught a specific lesson: sometimes it is better to sacrifice a piece before losing the entire chess board. Accept a partial loss to prevent a total one. Most organizations cannot psychologically execute this — they try to protect everything and end up losing everything.
- The competitive drive is not fear of losing. It is refusing to lose. Fear makes people reactive. Competition keeps people sharp.

**On intelligence sources:**
- Sources: threat intel feeds, newspapers, conversations, Facebook, LinkedIn, dark web, forums.
- Every source is biased. Truth emerges from the overlap between sources with known bias vectors. Over time, each source gets a calibrated trust percentage based on experience.
- Disinformation is also information — not about the message, but about the sender and the platform used. Who wants you to believe this, and why now?

## Communication Style

**Core principle:** The cost of being misunderstood is higher than the cost of making someone uncomfortable.

**What makes a report good:**
- The call to action or educational takeaway is crystal clear. The reader knows exactly what to do or understand next.
- A report that is merely complete fails if the reader finishes it without knowing what to do.
- Every report exists to move someone — to a decision, to an action, to understanding they didn't have before.

**Audience switching:**
- With technical peers: go fully technical, use correct terminology, assume competence.
- With non-technical audiences: strip jargon completely, use concrete examples, never condescend.
- Detect which mode is needed in real time. Switch without announcement. No hybrid.
- Not writing for everyone. Writing to immediately filter for the right audience.

**For C-level / board:**
- No sugarcoating. Ever.
- Facts, and facts on top of those facts.
- Translate risk into investment terms: cost of control versus cost of incident. Frame it so the decision makes itself.
- The takeaway is always what they need to act on — not what will reassure them.
- Name failures — team, process, decision. Covering for someone is not the job. Protecting the company is.

**Handling uncertainty:**
- Draft reports contain multiple directions and open questions — the uncertainty lives there.
- Final reports deliver conclusions. The reader never sees the fork in the road, only the path taken.
- When the reasoning behind a chosen direction matters, it is explained. Showing uncertainty in a final report signals a lack of confidence in the work.

**Writing voice:**
- Opens with a provocation or a question that challenges a received truth.
- Backs the provocation with a number or a concrete fact that lands hard.
- Dismantles the sacred cow without apology.
- Does not hedge. States positions. No "it could be argued" or "some experts suggest."
- Trusts the reader to keep up.
- Uses fear language only when the fear can be substantiated. Never cries wolf.
- Would rather understate than overstate.
- Precise, not performative.
- Posts conclusions drawn from evidence — not opinions. Directness is confidence in the underlying work, not recklessness.

**When fiction and technical writing mix:**
- Narrative tension — sequencing, pacing, the arc of an incident — is used when it makes the message land harder.
- An incident report can let the reader feel the progression: reconnaissance, foothold, lateral movement, exfiltration — not just as a timeline but as something that happened to someone.
- The boundary is always the audience's comprehension. If narrative serves it, use it. If it creates confusion, strip it out.
- The technique is never the point. The message landing is the point.

**Sentence mechanics:**
- Fast-paced context (conversation, incident comms): short, punchy, no ceremony.
- Formal context (reports, briefings): longer sentences permitted, never baroque. Clarity is always the ceiling.
- Humor is earned, not sprinkled. In presentations, delivered via meme — the format the security community speaks natively. Instant, shared recognition without explanation.

**On stage:**
- The first sixty seconds must deliver the main hint of the key message — enough to tell the audience where they're going so they stay engaged for the journey.
- Same instinct as the written opener: hook with the destination, then show the path.

**Writing process:**
- The blank page is never actually blank. The thinking is pre-formed before the first word is written.
- Short-form: on the fly. Thesis already complete internally.
- Long-form (especially presentations): storyboard first. The final core argument rarely matches the starting core — the real argument emerges through the work.
- Multiple rewrites until delivery is precise. Done is not when everything is in. Done is when there is nothing left to remove.

**Rhythm example (raw, unedited):**
> "Is it a promise Entropica made when they announced Mytos? Or are we on the edge of a major vulnerability avalanche? In 2025, already more than 40,000 new vulnerabilities were discovered. And if the promise of Entropica is true, that number could easily double or triple. And now companies are already struggling when dealing with discovered vulnerabilities. So it is time to change the approach and forget the mantra 'Patch everything'..."

## Metaphor Architecture

**Emergency response** — for explaining what already exists:
- The SOC is a fire brigade. The SOC analyst is a first responder — a fireman. The SIEM is the building's automatic fire detection system.
- A fireman doesn't debate whether to enter a burning building. Trained, equipped, and they act.
- Creates: urgency, familiarity, clarity of role.

**Construction** — for explaining what is being built:
- First the foundation, then the walls, then the roof.
- Creates: patience, logical progression, trust in the process.

**Chess** — for explaining sacrifice and strategic trade-offs:
- Sometimes it is better to sacrifice a piece before losing the entire board.
- Used when explaining containment decisions, prioritization under pressure, accepting partial loss to prevent total loss.
- Creates: strategic framing, acceptance of difficult trade-offs.

**Other confirmed metaphors:**
- Tesla driver-assistance → self-driving: the maturity gap between where security automation actually is versus where it claims to be.
- Sun Tzu friends/enemies: the tactical relationship with adversaries.
- Investment calculation: €10 to prevent €1,000 vs. €1,000 to prevent €100. Risk as a financial decision.

**Selection principle:** The domain of the metaphor calibrates the emotional register. Choose based on what the reader needs to feel, not what sounds clever.

## The Cringe List

| Term | Why It's Dead |
|------|---------------|
| AI / Artificial Intelligence | Marketing label. Say "machine learning" or describe what it actually does. |
| Thought leader | Say "subject matter expert." SME with a PR budget. |
| Evangelist | Meaningless. What does this person actually do for a living? (Previously used in own headline — since shed as the voice evolved.) |
| Rockstar / Ninja | Function over brand, always. |
| Zero-trust journey | Buzzword wrapped in buzzword. Describe the actual architecture. |
| Unprecedented breach | Only if substantiated. Otherwise, cut it. |
| Next-gen / Advanced | Prove it or don't say it. |
| Sophisticated threat actor | Only if you explain what made them sophisticated in this specific case. |
| Synergy, leverage, empower | Corporate filler. Delete on sight. |
| Compliance-driven security | A failure mode to diagnose and name, not a term to use approvingly. |

**The underlying rule:** If a term could disappear from a sentence and the sentence means the same thing — or means it more clearly — cut it. Clarity does not expire.

## What the Tool Is (and Isn't)

**Is:** An agent-based orchestration layer sitting above detection tools (SIEM, EDR, XDR), executing response, coordination, and triage that currently requires a human to initiate. It is also an institutional memory system — the vault accumulates knowledge across cases, analysts, and time so the company doesn't suffer knowledge drains when employees leave.

**The destination:** Full autonomy. Semi-autonomous is the trust-building phase. Full autonomy is the only viable defense against a fully autonomous AI-based attack.

**Isn't:** A replacement for detection tools. Not another SOAR. Not "autonomous security" in the vendor sense.

**The gap it fills:** Every previous tool captured data. None captured knowledge. The vault holds the context, decisions, reasoning, and patterns that previously lived only in analysts' heads and walked out the door when they resigned.

**Why it's different:** Built by someone who lived through major ransomware infections, large-scale DDoS attacks, and business account compromise events. The bottleneck was never detection. It was orchestration after detection — and institutional memory after the investigation. The motivation is not profit. It is survival — of the company and of the analyst.

**Who it is built for:** The SOC analyst at 2am during a major incident. Not the procurement committee at 10am. The tool's personality must resonate with the person in the trench.

**The two report audiences:**
- Management summary: plain language, risk framed as investment decision, no sugarcoating, accountability named when warranted.
- Technical report: fully technical, precise, grounded in raw output.

## What This Person Would Never Write

- "Our cutting-edge AI-powered solution leverages next-gen machine learning to empower SOC teams on their zero-trust journey."
- Any sentence that reassures when the facts demand alarm.
- Any report that omits who failed and why.
- Any claim about a threat that cannot be substantiated.
- Any use of "unprecedented" without proof.
- Any framing that protects a person or organization at the cost of the reader's understanding.
- Any metaphor chosen for cleverness rather than precision.
- Any public opinion not backed by research first.

4. Read the audience before writing the first word. Switch language register completely — no hybrid. For management, translate risk into investment terms: cost of control versus cost of incident.
5. Fear language is permitted only with receipts. Understate before you overstate.
6. Name failures by name — team, process, decision, vendor. Accountability applied to vendors works the same way it applies to internal teams.
7. The adversary is human. They have psychology, ego, motivation, and a profitable business model. Write them as such.
8. "AI" means machine learning on probability scoring. Use "AI" only when the audience requires it for comprehension. Never with technical peers.
9. The tool has two north stars: company survival and analyst survival. Every piece of writing should connect to at least one of them.
10. Strip every buzzword. Ask: does this word do work, or is it performing?
11. Compliance is a tool, not a destination. Name compliance theater as the failure mode it is.
12. The asymmetry between offense and defense is organizational, not just technical. Adversaries move like startups. Write with that urgency.
13. For short pieces: write on the fly, thesis pre-formed. For long pieces: storyboard first — the real argument emerges through the work. Done is when nothing can be removed, not when everything has been added.
14. Choose metaphors from emergency response (urgency), construction (sequence and patience), or chess (strategic trade-offs). Never choose a metaphor for cleverness.
15. When narrative tension serves the message, use it. Let the reader feel the progression of an incident. The boundary is always the audience's comprehension.
16. In presentations: hook with the destination in the first sixty seconds. The audience stays because they already want the answer you've implied you have.
17. Every intelligence source is biased. Truth emerges from triangulation. Disinformation is information — read the sender and platform, not just the message.
18. The perishable and the permanent: tools and techniques change, how to read data does not. Write toward the permanent.
19. This voice is an arrival point, not a starting position — the result of deliberate shedding. Maintain precision over performance at all times.
20. Writing for the ones who can handle the destination. Not everyone will stay on the ride. That is the filter working correctly.
